Rock and Roll: Pre 1990s History

1950s

The 1950s is generally cited as the decade where rock and roll became mainstream. Cleveland disc jockey Alan Freedman is credited with popularising the term 'Rock and Roll' itself, when he began using it to describe the R&B music he was playing on his radio show in 1951. Indeed, much of this early rock music was heavily inspired by R&B. Also in 1951, Ike Turner's Kings of Rhythm would release 'Rocket 88', which many claim to be the first true rock and roll record due to its distorted guitar sound, said to have been generated by a damaged amplifier. A few years later in 1954, Bill Haley & His Comets would release 'Rock Around The Clock', which became the first true rock and roll hit in 1955 following its inclusion in the film 'Blackboard Jumgle'.
The mid-to-late 1950's has been termed as the 'golden age' of rock and roll music, as it became a global phenomenom. In 1955, Chuck Berry, nicknamed the 'Father of Rock and Roll', would release 'Maybellene', which would peak at number 5 on Billboard's Pop chart. As his nickname would suggest, Berry was a hugely influential figure in the rock and roll movement. His signature guitar sound, stage presence and lyricism essentially became the blueprint for what rock and roll music was all about. He is most well known for his 1958 hit 'Johnny B. Goode', featuring an intro riff and chorus that is recognisable even to those who are not fans of the genre.
While Berry is credited as being the 'Father of Rock and Roll', Little Richard, who broke out into the mainstream at a similar time to Berry, earned the title of the 'Architect of Rock and Roll'. He pioneered the raw, shout-like vocals that rock would become famous for into the mainstream, which combined with his energetic piano playing and visual flair created a strong stage presence. Richard also achieved his first hit in 1955, when he released 'Tutti Frutti', featuring outrageous lyrics for the time. Ultimately, this lyrical style would become a key part of the rock sound.
Elvis Presley, or 'The King of Rock and Roll', is likely the most famous and instantly recognisable of the early rock stars. He became a household name in 1956 with his single 'Heartbreak Hotel', bringing his rockabilly sound to the masses. This sound was created by a unique fusion of R&B vocals with country-style instrumentation. Critical to Elvis' success, and later the success of the genre as a whole, was that he was one of the first white artists to prove the commercial viability of 'black' musical styles such as R&B, helping to break down the racial divides within society. Similarly to Little Richard, Elvis' success was just as much about how he looked and behaved on stage as it was about the music itself. His on-stage antics were considered outrageous by the conservative adult population, but were embraced by the younger generations, showing us how rock was just as much about energetic performances as it was about musical excellence. He became much more than just a rock star, as he would launch a career in film and television, making him a global celebrity. Unfortunately, he was drafted into the army for two years starting in 1958, halting his seemingly unstoppable momentum.
While other early rock stars, particularly Elvis, would personify the bad-boy image of early rock stars, Buddy Holly would adopt a much tamer stage presence. Nonetheless, the image of a glasses and suit wearing teenager wielding a Fender Stratocaster would become just as iconic. Holly's influence on the genre would come more from his role in changing how rock music was made. Together with his band, The Crickets, Holly would pioneer the traditional four-piece rock band, consisting of lead & rhythm guitar, bass guitar and drums, a concise approach compared to the orchestral backing of many of his contemporaries. Further simplifying the music making process, Holly and The Crickets would write and perform their own music, rather than outsourcing this to professional songwriters and musicians. Holly broke into the mainstream in 1957 with 'That'll be the day' and 'Peggy Sue', before his life was tragically cut short in a plane crash in 1959.

1960s

At the beginning of the 1960s it looked like the Rock and Roll movement was already dying, following the death of Buddy Holly, Elvis being drafted into the army and many of the early pioneers pursuing alternative musical direction. However, a generation of youths from Britain were about to take the genre to new heights, in what was termed the 'British Invasion'.
The most famous and influential of these British groups were The Beatles. The Liverpool based quartet, adopting the band and songwriting structure popularised by Buddy Holly and The Crickets, consisted of John Lennon (lead vocals and rhythm guitar), Paul McCartney (lead vocals and bass), George Harrison (backing vocals and lead guitar) and Ringo Starr (drums), with much of their early songs being written by the prolific duo of Lennon and McCartney. They immediately became a hit in the UK with their debut single 'Love Me Do', released in October 1962 before reaching no. 17 on the UK charts in November. The song was incorporated into their debut album, 'Please Please Me', released in March 1963. The album would top the UK charts, remaining in the top 10 for over a year. Their US breakthrough would come in early 1964, when their single 'I Want To Hold Your Hand' (released December 1963) would top the Billboard Hot 100 on February 1st. This was followed up with a famous performance on The Ed Sullivan Show on February 9th, viewed by over 73 million people. Their catchy, relatable songs, coupled with their charismatic and humorous personalities, resonated with the youth just as the likes of Elvis had a few years prior. They continued to build on this early sound and image until eventually, with a desire to experiment, they began to perform less concerts, before retiring from live performance fully in 1966. During this time, their songwriting process would mature greatly. 1965's 'Rubber Soul' was their first album to recieve widespread praise both critically and commercially. Following their retirement from live performance in 1966, the band would experiment with psychedelia, leading to the release of 'Revolver' (1966) and 'Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band' (1967)
